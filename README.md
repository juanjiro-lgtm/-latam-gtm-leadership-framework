Scaling Technology Across Latin America

A practical GTM leadership framework by Juan Manuel Jiménez

I am a technology sales and go-to-market leader with more than two decades of experience building businesses, developing teams, and accelerating enterprise technology adoption across Latin America.

My career has been shaped by a consistent challenge:

How do you translate a global technology strategy into a scalable, locally relevant, and commercially successful operation across Latin America?

Latin America is not one market. It is a collection of countries with different levels of technology maturity, buying behaviors, regulatory environments, partner ecosystems, currencies, cultures, and approaches to risk.

Successfully scaling technology in the region requires more than translating a global playbook into Spanish or Portuguese. It requires local judgment, disciplined execution, strong talent, and the ability to create trust.

This repository presents the principles and operating framework I use to lead technology go-to-market organizations across LATAM.

1. Treat LATAM as a portfolio of markets

One of the most common GTM mistakes is treating Latin America as a single, homogeneous territory.

A strong LATAM strategy evaluates countries using multiple dimensions:

Dimension	Questions
Market potential	Is there sufficient demand and addressable revenue?
Problem intensity	Is the customer problem urgent enough to fund?
Technology maturity	Are customers ready to adopt the solution?
Competitive landscape	Are we creating a category or displacing an incumbent?
Local execution capacity	Do we have talent, partners, references, and support?
Commercial friction	How difficult are contracting, currency, procurement, and compliance?
Expansion potential	Can an initial customer become a regional platform?

Countries should then be classified into clear investment tiers.

Tier 1: Build directly

Markets with sufficient demand, talent availability, customer maturity, and long-term strategic potential.

Tier 2: Scale through a hybrid model

Markets that justify focused direct coverage supported by strong local partners.

Tier 3: Develop through partners

Markets where initial growth should be partner-led until demand and repeatability justify greater direct investment.

The goal is not equal coverage. The goal is intentional coverage.

2. Start with expensive customer problems

Technology does not create urgency by itself.

Customers act when a problem is sufficiently visible, costly, strategic, or risky.

Before positioning a product, I ask:

* What business problem are we solving?
* Who personally owns that problem?
* Why does it matter now?
* What happens if the customer does nothing?
* Which executive metric is affected?
* Is there an existing budget?
* Can the customer quantify the cost of the current situation?
* Is the organization capable of acting on the insight the technology provides?

This prevents sales teams from becoming feature presenters.

The strongest LATAM opportunities generally connect technology to one or more executive priorities:

* Revenue growth
* Customer retention
* Cost reduction
* Employee productivity
* Risk reduction
* Brand differentiation
* Operational consistency
* Faster decision-making
* Better customer or employee experiences

The commercial conversation must move from:

“Here is what the platform does.”

to:

“Here is the business outcome we can create, how we will measure it, and why acting now matters.”

3. Localize the value, not only the language

Localization is not translation.

A global value proposition must be adapted to local economic conditions, organizational maturity, buying processes, industry structures, and executive priorities.

For example, the same platform may be positioned differently depending on the market:

* In a mature enterprise, the priority may be consolidation, governance, and AI-enabled scale.
* In a fast-growing regional company, it may be speed, standardization, and visibility.
* In a cost-sensitive organization, the business case may focus on efficiency and rapid time to value.
* In a regulated industry, trust, security, compliance, and risk may dominate the decision.

An effective localized value proposition answers five questions:

1. Why this problem?
2. Why now?
3. Why this solution?
4. Why our company?
5. Why should the customer believe us?

4. Build a leadership system, not a collection of individual sellers

Sustainable regional growth cannot depend on a few heroic performers.

The leader’s responsibility is to build a system in which performance can be repeated.

My leadership model is based on four principles.

Clarity

Every person should understand:

* Their market
* Their ideal customer profile
* Their sales priorities
* Their expected behaviors
* Their performance metrics
* Their role within the broader regional strategy

Coaching

Managers should not function only as forecast administrators.

They must develop judgment, commercial thinking, deal strategy, executive presence, and leadership capability.

Accountability

Accountability requires clear expectations, transparent inspection, early identification of risk, and direct conversations.

It should not depend on end-of-quarter pressure.

Talent multiplication

The strongest sign of leadership is not only personal performance. It is the number of people who become more capable, confident, and successful under that leadership.

I measure leadership success through:

* Business results
* Quality of execution
* Team engagement
* Internal promotions
* Talent retention
* Forecast accuracy
* Customer outcomes
* The strength of the next generation of leaders

5. Use partners as a growth multiplier

LATAM cannot always be scaled efficiently through direct headcount alone.

Partners can provide:

* Local credibility
* Market access
* Industry expertise
* Implementation capacity
* Customer relationships
* Geographic coverage
* Local contracting capabilities
* Complementary services

However, signing many partners is not the same as having a partner strategy.

A productive partner ecosystem requires:

1. Clear market and customer focus
2. Defined roles and commercial rules
3. Joint account planning
4. Practical enablement
5. Executive sponsorship
6. Shared pipeline accountability
7. Delivery capability
8. Transparent attribution
9. A repeatable customer value proposition

My preferred model is based on quality rather than quantity.

A small number of committed, capable, and accountable partners will usually create more value than a large inactive network.

Partners should not simply receive leads. They should contribute insight, relationships, implementation capabilities, and market creation.

6. Operate the business through leading indicators

Revenue is the final result. It is not the only operating metric.

By the time a revenue problem becomes visible, it is often too late to correct it.

A strong GTM operating system reviews leading indicators such as:

Area	Example indicators
Market creation	Target-account engagement, executive conversations, partner-sourced demand
Pipeline	Qualified pipeline creation, coverage, stage progression, deal aging
Sales quality	Problem validation, executive sponsorship, quantified value, decision process
Conversion	Stage-to-stage conversion, win rate, competitive performance
Velocity	Sales-cycle duration, time between milestones, stalled opportunities
Forecasting	Accuracy, risk identification, next-step quality
Talent	Ramp progress, coaching activity, performance distribution
Partners	Active partners, sourced pipeline, influenced pipeline, joint opportunities
Customers	Adoption, outcomes, references, expansion potential

The objective is not to create excessive reporting.

The objective is to identify where execution is breaking before the final result is affected.

7. Convert regional learning into scale

Every customer interaction should improve the regional operating system.

Teams should continuously capture:

* Common customer problems
* Industry-specific use cases
* Successful executive messages
* Objection patterns
* Competitive insights
* Procurement barriers
* Partner strengths
* Implementation risks
* Expansion opportunities
* Customer proof points

Those insights should then influence:

* Product feedback
* Marketing programs
* Sales enablement
* Partner strategy
* Hiring priorities
* Customer success
* Territory design
* Future market investment

A regional organization becomes scalable when learning moves faster than individual experience.

My GTM Operating Rhythm

A strong strategy requires a consistent operating cadence.

Weekly

* Review material deals and pipeline changes.
* Identify opportunities requiring executive support.
* Inspect next steps, decision processes, and deal risk.
* Coach managers and sellers.
* Review new pipeline creation.
* Surface customer, product, and competitive insights.

Monthly

* Review country and segment performance.
* Evaluate conversion, sales velocity, and forecast accuracy.
* Assess partner contribution and execution.
* Review talent development and hiring.
* Identify repeated patterns across markets.
* Align regional priorities with global stakeholders.

Quarterly

* Reassess market potential and investment priorities.
* Review territory and coverage design.
* Evaluate the ideal customer profile.
* Inspect resource allocation.
* Update the regional partner strategy.
* Review leadership succession and talent development.
* Translate regional learning into the next GTM iteration.

Leading AI and Emerging Technology in LATAM

Artificial intelligence creates an important opportunity for Latin America, but adoption will not be driven by novelty alone.

Successful AI commercialization requires trust and practical business value.

A strong AI GTM motion should demonstrate:

* A clearly defined business problem
* Measurable economic or operational value
* Fast time to value
* Responsible use of data
* Security and governance
* Integration into existing workflows
* Executive sponsorship
* User adoption
* A path from initial use case to enterprise scale

For companies building new AI categories, regional leadership also requires market education.

The GTM team must help customers understand:

* What is now possible
* Which use cases are commercially relevant
* How AI changes existing workflows
* Where human judgment remains essential
* How to experiment without creating uncontrolled risk
* How to move from pilot projects to scalable adoption

My approach is to combine vision with execution:

Inspire customers with what technology can make possible, but earn their trust through measurable outcomes, disciplined implementation, and local relevance.
